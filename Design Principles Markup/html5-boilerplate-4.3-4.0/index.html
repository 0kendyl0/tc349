Good and Bad Webdesign Principle Examples

Rule of Thirds

Good: Cengage has good use of Rule of Thirds as everything formatted on the page follows that design principle strictly.

Bad: Amazon does not follow Rule of Thirds very well as everything seems randomly placed in boxes all around the site.

Golden Ratio

Good: HarperCollins has consistent and appealing spacing between all its elements on the page.

Bad: Harlequin has a site that is so poorly formatted that the Golden Ratio is not even apparent in any of its elements.

Ockham's Razor

Good: Simon and Schuster has a site that is simplistic without being barren. The whole point is less is more, which really works here.

Bad: Goodreads site is not completely hopeless, but a simpler design could make the site more functional and logical.

Modularity

Good: Penguin really uses modularity to its advantage with every different element being in its own box. It has very nice flow.

Bad: Barnes and Noble does use modularity on its site, but it has way too many modules that end up doing the same thing. It is too much for the site to function well for the average user.

Signal-to-Noise Ratio

Good: Random House has enough negative to positive space that it makes it easy for the user to find what they are looking for.

Bad: Schuler Books site is too noisy with the color and image choices for the site. It needs to be toned down to work for users.
